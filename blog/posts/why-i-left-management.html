Why I walked away from management to write code

June 2025

In 2021 I was a store manager. I had a team and a branch I'd helped open. It was the aftermath of COVID19, we had pushed through the lockdown and everything was going back to normal. I was exhausted, but it felt like a real achievement. We had made it through the worst of the pandemic and I had done a good job leading my team through it. I thrived during the chrisis, but when things settled down I found myself asking: is this what I want to do long-term?

So I made the decision that any sane person would make in that situation: I stepped down from my management role and went back to being a customer advisor, so I could study for a degree in Software Engineering. It was a big change, but it felt right. I wanted to push myself to do more and give myself the chance to build things, not just manage them.

What management actually taught me

I spent four years in retail management at Argos. The last big thing I did was help with the setup and opening of a new branch with a couple of co-workers — recruitment, training, launch operations, all of it. That was genuinely satisfying work. I found I thrived on fixing things.

But the thing I kept noticing, throughout all of it, was that the most interesting problems were the system problems. Why does this process break down at this point? They've updated this system in a way thats not best for the end user. What's the actual constraint here, and how do you remove it?

If I knew how to code, I could fix this problem in a much better way.

What the degree was actually like

Southampton Solent's Software Engineering course was practical in a way I appreciated. Most modules involved creating some sort of artifact alongside the theory, whether that was a design document, a testing suite, or an actual piece of software. My final year dissertation I made a full mobile app, built from scratch.

I designed and built it entirely myself, from the Figma wireframes through to the React Native implementation, SQLite data layer, and usability testing with real users. It got a first, but more importantly it confirmed that I could actually do this — take an idea, make the decisions, and ship something real.

What management still gives me

A few things from those years have turned out to be genuinely useful as a developer:

  • I'm comfortable with ownership. When something is your responsibility, you fix it. That mindset transfers.
  • I know how to talk to non-technical people. Years of translating between different teams means I can explain technical decisions without being condescending about it.
  • I understand that software exists to solve real problems. The best systems I saw in retail were the ones that made someone's actual job easier. That's what good software should do.
  • I don't panic when things go wrong. Managing a store through COVID-19 recalibrates what counts as a crisis.

Where I'm at now

I graduated in 2025 with a first-class degree and I'm now looking for junior engineering roles. I'm still actively building — Lone Scribe, the redesign of my dissertation app, is an ongoing project that I'm expanding in my own time.

The career change was the right call. I'm more engaged by this work than I've been by anything else. If you're considering something similar and want to talk through it, feel free to get in touch.
